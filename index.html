Permission Ticket

I used to think transitioning into adulthood would involve far more official correspondence than it does. As a child, everything required permission. School picnics, playing games, meeting friends. Someone older had to sign a piece of paper confirming that yes, you were allowed to leave the building, ride the bus, eat questionable food in the canteen, and return hopefully with all your limbs. The strange part is that the paperwork disappears long before the instinct does. Nobody tells you that one day you'll simply be expected to decide where to live, who to love, what work to do, whether to have children, whether not to have children, whether to move countries, leave a marriage, start a company, or shave your head on a Wednesday afternoon because you're done looking at the hairball or curious what your scalp actually look like. There isn't a committee. There isn't an interview. No one appears at your front door with a clipboard and says, "Congratulations. We reviewed your application and are pleased to inform you that you're now qualified to begin living in a way that feels authentic," which is unfortunate because I think an alarming number of us are still waiting for exactly that. Not consciously, of course. Nobody would admit to it.

We call it being sensible. Instead, we say we're gathering experience, building confidence, getting our ducks in a row, despite the fact that no one has ever explained why ducks are involved in any of this, or why they apparently refuse to stand where they're supposed to.

It sounds adult, mature…… maybe even responsible. I just want to be sure. It's a perfectly respectable sentence, almost impossible to argue with. Who the hell doesn't want to be sure? The difficulty is that certainty has become one of those things people insist exists despite very little evidence. It's a bit like closure or inbox zero. Everyone talks about it as though someone somewhere has achieved it. I've yet to meet them. I think we're all carrying around an imaginary audience. Not a hostile one. Quite the opposite actually. A deeply qualified one. They wear sensible shoes, understand taxes, FDs. They never panic in metro station in the early rush, for reasons they themselves can't explain. They know how pensions work. Their herbs stay alive. And for some reason, we've decided they are watching. Watching us consider searching for jobs or watching us think about moving out and staying alone. We even think about them watching us wonder in the future whether 40 is too late to start writing or 32 too early to change careers or whether it's socially acceptable to become the kind of person who says yes to things without first consulting 17 relatives and friends in an Excel spreadsheet.

It's almost as though we're waiting for one of them to lean over and say, "Actually, I think you'd be rather good at this." Only then would it feel legitimate. Only then would we have permission.

The problem, of course, is that these people don't exist, or rather they do exist. But they're far too busy wondering whether someone else approves of them to spend any time thinking about you, which is either devastating or incredibly freeing depending on the day. A few months ago, I became mildly obsessed with the idea that there must be a point at which people begin feeling like proper adults. Not the performative version, the one where you buy a house plant and suddenly develop strong opinions about money plant, but the internal shift. The moment someone wakes up and thinks, "Right, I know what I'm doing now." I started asking people, my close friends, people younger than me, people older than me, smarter than me, people with impressive jobs, and who appeared to own matching towels. Almost every answer was some variation of the same thing. They were making it up. Not viciously or incompetently (idk if the spelling is right, but nvm). They, too, were improvising.

One friend told me she'd spent her college years convinced someone would eventually realize she'd accidentally been put in charge. Another admitted she still looked around in interviews and meetings expecting the real adult to arrive. A man who had successfully built and started selling custom sensor modules confessed that every major decision of his life had felt suspiciously premature.

None of this was particularly comforting at the time and wanted one of them to tell me there was a moment, a ceremony, certificate, maybe a secret password. Instead, they kept describing something much less glamorous. They described deciding anyway.

I wonder if that's the part we've misunderstood. We think confidence grants permission, as though confidence is the ticket you collect before boarding the train. But perhaps confidence is simply what accumulates after you spend enough time traveling, doing shit all alone. It isn't the prerequisite. It's the receipt. That would explain why the people who appear so certain now often describe their beginnings with words like accidental, chaotic, embarrassing, or lucky. Very few people tell stories that begin, "I felt completely prepared." Most stories worth hearing begin with someone feeling slightly ridiculous. Someone sending the text before they could talk themselves out of it, booking the flat, and immediately checking the agreement form after signing, introducing themselves as a writer while secretly hoping nobody asked too many follow-up questions. From the outside, these moments become origin stories. From the inside, they mostly feel like administrative errors. I've noticed something else, too. Whenever people describe the lives they wish they had, they rarely describe extraordinary things. Not really. They talk about small versions of themselves.

The version who hosts friends without apologizing for the state of the house. The one who wears the clothes they are saving for special occasions or who stops ordering the safest thing on the menu. The version who finally learns Kannada because they've been threatening to do it for 5 years and are beginning to suspect Duolingo isn't going to drag them there against their will. Although I'm still very suspicious of that owl. The version who applies, asks, says the thing, who stops rehearsing conversations and just starts having them. I remember myself thinking of the day when I’ll be away from family, living the way I want, un-questioned. But when it’s time, it’s overwhelming. It's crazy how often the life we're longing for is separated from the one we're living by decisions so ordinary they barely qualify as plot. And yet, we keep treating them as though they're waiting on approval from some invisible office. What would you do if nobody had an opinion? It's one of those questions that sounds pleasantly philosophical until you actually attempt to answer it. Because at first you think everything: I'd write differently, dress differently, speak more honestly, consume less energy drinks……. then after a while something slightly more uncomfortable appears. Would I? Or have I become so accustomed to imagining the audience that I've started performing even when the theater is empty? It's a strange thought. How much of your personality is preference and how much is anticipation? How many decisions have you outsourced to people who were never consulted in the first place? Perhaps that's what we're really asking for when we say we need permission, not approval. Relief. Relief from the responsibility of choosing.

Because once you admit no one is coming to decide for you, life becomes wonderfully open and terribly inconvenient at exactly the same time.

You can't blame timing forever or circumstances or the fact that you weren't ready. Sometimes those things are true. Of course, life is complicated. Money matters. Health matters. Because this isn't an argument for reckless optimism disguised as wisdom, but there is a difference between a genuine obstacle and an imaginary authority. One can stop you. The other only asks that you believe it exists. So maybe this is as close to a permission slip as anyone can offer. Not because I have any particular authority. If anything, I've become increasingly suspicious of people who seem very certain they're qualified to tell everyone else how to live. But because every interesting person I've ever met eventually discovered the same mildly disappointing truth, nobody picked them or announced it was their turn. Nobody called to say they'd finally become convincing enough to begin. They simply reached the point where waiting started to feel more absurd than trying. And maybe that's adulthood. Not knowing exactly what you're doing or feeling fearless. Not becoming some polished, fully realized version of yourself who never second guesses anything. Just slowly accepting that the permission you've been waiting for was never delayed. It was never coming, which sounds initially like terrible news until you realize it also means no one can withhold it.

You don't need to earn your place in your own life. You only have to stop standing outside it politely knocking convinced someone else is supposed to answer the door.
